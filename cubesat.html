Gorgon CubeSat

The Gorgon CubeSat is a proposed 12U CubeSat mission I’ve been developing since my time with Arizona State University’s Sun Devil Satellite Laboratory (SDSL) in 2023, where my talented team of engineering students laid the groundwork with significant contributions. After leaving SDSL, I’ve continued refining this concept independently, aiming to catalog ocean plastic pollution from orbit using a hyperspectral camera in the 1000-1700 nm range, targeting macroplastics in zones like the Great Pacific Garbage Patch. The secondary goal is to integrate a modified Starlink ground terminal for data relay to Starlink satellites, eliminating traditional ground stations. This research-based proposal envisions scalable marine monitoring, though it remains purely conceptual with no simulations or hardware development yet undertaken.

Problem Statement
Ocean plastic pollution is a critical issue, with 8-12 million tonnes entering annually, primarily as macroplastics that threaten marine ecosystems through entanglement and ingestion. Current monitoring—limited to in-situ surveys and aerial observations—falls short of the coverage needed to address this crisis. The “Missing Plastic Problem” reveals only 269,000 tonnes on the surface versus millions inputted yearly, underscoring the need for advanced remote sensing to map distribution and explore sinks.

Approach
At SDSL, our team collaboratively explored CubeSat concepts for environmental monitoring, with my peers contributing key insights into payload and orbit design. Post-SDSL, I’ve expanded this into a research proposal featuring a hyperspectral camera for plastic detection, leveraging SWIR absorption peaks (e.g., 1215 nm, 1410 nm) against water’s low reflectance. The innovative addition of a Starlink terminal for space-to-space communication emerged from my independent research, inspired by its bandwidth potential. This remains a theoretical framework, drawing from literature and existing technologies.

Design and Research
The proposed 12U platform (20x20x30 cm, ~24 kg) includes:
- Hyperspectral Camera: A custom SWIR imager (1000-1700 nm, 10 nm resolution, 50 m GSD, 50 km swath), inspired by Headwall Photonics’ Hyperspec SWIR, sized at 2U, 15 W—conceptual only.
- ADCS: Blue Canyon XACT-50 (0.5U, 0.85 kg, 5 W), targeting <0.1° pointing accuracy—placeholder based on specs.
- OBC: Unibap iX5-100 (9x9x2 cm, 0.2 kg, 3 W), for potential onboard processing—researched, not implemented.
- EPS: DHV Technology 12U solar panels (25 W, 1.5 kg) and GOMspace NanoPower BPX battery (100 Wh, 0.8 kg)—theoretical power design.
- Communications: A modified Starlink terminal (1.5U, 2.5 kg, 20 W, Ku-band, 100 Mbps)—a conceptual adaptation requiring development.
- Structure: ISISpace 12U frame (1.2 kg) with passive thermal control—standard design consideration.
No modeling or simulations have been performed; this is a research synthesis of potential components.

Feasibility and Safety
The 12U size supports the proposed payload and subsystems, aligning with rideshare launch options. The hyperspectral camera’s 50 m GSD is theoretically viable at 500 km altitude, and the Starlink terminal concept leverages existing technology, though it demands space qualification and regulatory approval—unproven without testing. As a research proposal, it poses no physical risks, with feasibility resting on future hardware realization.

Results and Impact
As a conceptual proposal, no results exist, but it envisions mapping macroplastics across 50 km swaths to address the “Missing Plastic” issue. The Starlink relay could potentially offer 100 Mbps downlink, bypassing ground stations if feasible—though this remains speculative. If executed, Gorgon could enhance marine pollution monitoring, but its impact hinges on transitioning from research to development, a step yet to be taken.
