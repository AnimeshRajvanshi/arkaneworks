Mechanical Wrong-Way Driving Prevention System

The Mechanical Wrong-Way Driving Prevention System, developed during my 2024 Mechanical Engineering capstone at Arizona State University, mitigates the lethal risks of wrong-way driving on highways. As part of a seven-member team, I collaborated on a speed hump system design, taking sole responsibility for its electrical components to alert impaired drivers through tactile and electronic feedback, enhancing safety without disrupting traffic.

Wrong-way driving accounts for 1% of highway accidents but has a 25% fatality rate, claiming 300-400 lives annually in Arizona (Arizona Department of Transportation). Predominantly caused by impaired drivers (65% of cases) at night, our challenge was to design a cost-effective, durable system. We settled on a pointed, hinged speed hump, using SolidWorks for CAD and ANSYS for FEA, transitioning from plywood to a $401.85 steel prototype within a $700 budget.

The system features steel plates forming depressible ramps with hinges and bolts, refined through team CAD efforts. I designed an Arduino-based electrical system with aluminum foil contact switches and LED alerts for direction detection. FEA confirmed safety factors of 2.5-3.3 under 1000 lbf, validated by low-speed tests. It withstands heavy use with springs and rollers, requiring minimal road changes.

Live demonstrations proved efficacy: mechanical humps jolted drivers, and my electrical circuit triggered LED signals accurately. With safety factors above 2.5 and 57.4% budget utilization, this scalable solution blends team mechanical design with my electrical innovation, poised to reduce fatalities. Future fatigue analysis is planned for higher speeds.
